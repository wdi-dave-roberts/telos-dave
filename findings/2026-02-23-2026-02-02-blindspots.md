* Blindspots in thinking about the Hierarchy of Care:

1. Failing to acknowledge Tonia's contributions and perspectives as equally valuable.
2. Assuming my own wiring is universal, neglecting individual differences.
3. Overemphasizing safety without considering other aspects, like connection or beauty.
4. Using the Hierarchy as a business framework rather than an ethics framework that guides all aspects of life.
5. Failing to recognize Tonia's love language (cuisine) and its importance in her relationships.
6. Not exploring how my own trauma has shaped my care hierarchy.
7. Assuming that others will understand and value the same hierarchy without explicit communication.
8. Neglecting the need for ongoing discussion, revision, and adaptation of the Hierarchy with Tonia's input.

* Blindspots in thinking about growth and difference:

1. Failing to acknowledge the possibility of personal growth and change over time.
2. Assuming that differences between partners are inherently problematic or threatening.
3. Not considering the potential benefits of complementary skills and perspectives.
4. Neglecting the importance of communication, compromise, and mutual understanding in navigating differences.
5. Failing to recognize the value of learning from each other's strengths and weaknesses.
6. Overemphasizing individual effort without acknowledging the impact of external factors on personal growth.
7. Not exploring how my own fears and doubts about our relationship may be influencing my perception of growth and difference.
8. Neglecting the importance of self-reflection, self-awareness, and emotional intelligence in navigating differences.

* Blindspots in thinking about identity and purpose:

1. Failing to acknowledge that personal growth and change can lead to new identities and purposes.
2. Assuming that one's current sense of purpose is fixed or unchanging.
3. Not considering the importance of exploration, experimentation, and taking risks in discovering one's true passions and values.
4. Overemphasizing external validation (e.g., reputation, reviews) over internal motivation and fulfillment.
5. Failing to recognize the role of trauma and adversity in shaping one's sense of purpose and identity.
6. Neglecting the importance of self-compassion, self-awareness, and emotional intelligence in navigating identity and purpose.
7. Not exploring how my own fears and doubts about our relationship may be influencing my perception of identity and purpose.
8. Failing to acknowledge that purpose can evolve over time, and that it's okay to change one's mind or direction.

* Blindspots in thinking about trauma and its expression:

1. Failing to recognize the possibility that trauma may manifest differently in individuals, even within a single family.
2. Assuming that my own trauma is universal or representative of others' experiences.
3. Not considering the importance of individualized care and support for those dealing with trauma.
4. Overemphasizing the role of personal responsibility or "getting over" trauma without acknowledging its ongoing impact.
5. Failing to recognize the value of expressing love and care through various means, including physical touch, emotional connection, and tangible actions.
6. Neglecting the importance of self-compassion, self-awareness, and emotional intelligence in navigating trauma's aftermath.
7. Not exploring how my own expression of trauma may be influencing others' perceptions or behaviors.
8. Failing to acknowledge that trauma can lead to new strengths and abilities, rather than solely deficits or limitations.
