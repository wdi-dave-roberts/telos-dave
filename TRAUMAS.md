# TRAUMAS

> *Past traumas — for context and healing*

---

## Origin

Tonia and I are high school sweethearts. Together since 15, now 56. Both from Evansville, IN — depressed, regressive town. Both from broken, abusive homes.

**Her:** Sexually abused by her father. Emotionally abused by family.

**Me:** Emotionally and physically abused.

We were broken, afraid kids when we found each other. But when we found each other, we found safety. A home.

Married immediately after high school. I joined the Air Force — the only way I knew to provide stability, to escape poverty. We shipped off, leaned on each other, never looked back.

---

## The Family Dysfunction

The dysfunction doesn't leave you because you leave the family. It did for me. Not for her.

Tonia has the most tender, sweet spirit. She doesn't give up on people, regardless if they give up on her. For 30+ years I watched her try to connect with her sister Rayanna, to be loved by her. And I watched Rayanna neglect and abuse the kindness. I tried repeatedly to get Tonia to let the relationship go. She couldn't.

---

## The Crucible

Three years ago, Tonia's mom Ruth got dementia, then Alzheimer's. Ruth was the kindest of the bunch — the closest thing to someone in her family of origin who loved her.

Tonia poured herself into her mom's care. But to do it, she had to endure her father — her abuser — being around him.

Her father also attempted to sexually abuse our daughter over the years. We had broken contact because of it. But when Ruth got sick, Tonia wasn't willing to abandon her mom. So we had to endure this monster.

I swallowed bitter, bitter anger. Because Ruth needed us. Because my wife wasn't willing to not be there for her mother. Deep, deep sacrifice.

Ruth slipped away slowly, painfully.

---

## The Catalyst

Less than a year after Ruth died, Rayanna was walking to her front porch to drink her morning coffee. She dropped dead of a heart attack. No illness. No warning. Like a finger of God — boom, gone.

Despite Tonia's conflicting feelings about her sister — the deep desire to be loved by her, the deep love for her — suddenly she was dead. At our age.

It shook us to our core.

---

## The Reckoning

That day — the very day we got the news — we looked at each other and knew. Our ultra-upper-middle-class existence could buy anything, do anything. But we couldn't say what we were living for. What we were adding to the world. Why we existed.

We'd always known that wasn't the life we wanted. That day, we both finally acknowledged it.

Eight months later, we owned the White Doe Inn.
